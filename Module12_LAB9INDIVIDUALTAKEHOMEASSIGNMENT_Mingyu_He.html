Analyze Every Stakeholder Group
1. ACADEMIC ADVISORS — Current workflow, core fear about AI, level of influence over adoption, and what success looks like for them 6 months post-launch:
Academic advisors currently work inside a high-friction registration and clearance workflow. In the current state, a typical advisor must verify student identity, review academic standing, scan holds, interpret degree audit results, compare course options, manage overrides, and document follow-up actions in Albert. During peak registration periods, advisors typically handle about 15–20 registration cases per week, and a standard advising session already takes 30–45 minutes before extra time is added for cross-department approvals or hold resolution. Advisors often keep parallel notes or trackers because the current workflow is fragmented across multiple screens and tabs.

Their core fear about AI is not that “AI exists,” but that AI will create faster but less defensible advising. Advisors will resist adoption if Albert produces recommendations that ignore exceptions, transfer credits, milestone nuances, or unclear hold ownership because that forces the advisor to recheck everything manually and increases student-facing risk. Advisors have very high influence over adoption because they are the frontline interpreters of Albert for students. If they do not trust the redesigned workflow, they will revert to email, spreadsheets, and manual workarounds, which would quietly undermine the rollout. Six months post-launch, success means advisors can identify high-risk students faster, see next-term registration gaps and active holds in one place, reduce manual tracking, and spend more time on proactive intervention instead of rule clarification. Adoption will be considered healthy only if weekly dashboard usage exceeds 85%, core dashboard load time remains under 2 seconds, and critical alerts are delivered within 5 minutes of the trigger.
2. REGISTRAR & BURSAR STAFF — Specific workflow disruptions during transition, concerns about the new system, what they need to feel confident before go-live:
Registrar staff currently work in a high-volume, time-sensitive operational workflow centered on course capacity, waitlists, overrides, and enrollment exceptions. During peak registration, they may handle 30–50 emails per day and review override requests in batches several times daily. Their workflow requires them to identify the real blocker behind a request, since many “class is full” complaints are actually caused by holds, permissions, prereqs, or time conflicts. They also must decide when to raise capacity, when to keep caps fixed, and when to process graduation-critical exceptions, often with room, faculty workload, and fairness constraints in mind.

Their main concern during transition is operational slowdown. If the redesigned Albert adds clicks, creates duplicate queues, obscures the actual blocker, or weakens auditability, staff will see the new system as making their job harder. Before go-live, they need confidence in three specific areas: first, the system must correctly identify and display the primary blocker in real time; second, override queues, waitlists, and demand pressure must refresh quickly enough for operational use; and third, every decision must remain traceable with clear status and escalation paths. Their confidence threshold should be explicit: before wider rollout, the pilot must show registrar staff weekly usage >90%, dashboard load time <2 seconds, real-time refresh latency <30 seconds, and alert delivery time <5 minutes. Full rollout should be delayed if override processing time worsens, if unresolved pending overrides exceed 20 requests, or if waitlist and blocker routing accuracy drops below acceptable operational quality.
3. STUDENTS (as stakeholders) — Their core concern about AI handling sensitive data. How does their trust need to be earned — specifically, through design and communication?
Students are stakeholders because they will decide very quickly whether the redesigned Albert feels more trustworthy than the old one. Their core concern is that AI may interpret sensitive academic information incorrectly, present a recommendation as if it were final, or use data they do not understand without giving them a clear explanation. Student trust will not be earned by saying “Albert is smarter now.” It must be earned through specific design choices: plain-language explanations of holds and blockers, visible distinction between “system guidance” and “staff decision,” clear next steps, and a visible escalation path when human review is required.

Trust also depends on communication. Students should never first encounter the redesign at the exact moment they are trying to register. Before go-live, students need to hear that the new Albert is intended to make registration blockers, requirements, and next actions easier to understand while preserving staff review for high-stakes cases. The design standard should be that a student can identify what is blocking them, which office owns the issue, what action they must take, and whether the case is pending human review within one guided session. If student help tickets about “I don’t understand why I’m blocked” do not decline by at least 20% by month 3, the communication and interface need revision before broader expansion. This target is a recommended change-management threshold derived from the existing advising and blocker-resolution workflows.
4. IT DEPARTMENT — Technical concerns about maintaining an AI-integrated system. What do they need from you before they will support the project?
The IT department’s role is to decide whether Albert can be operated reliably at scale, not just whether the redesign is conceptually attractive. IT will focus on system stability, refresh reliability, integration quality, supportability, and access governance. Based on the existing role dashboards and workflow documents, the redesigned Albert will need to support real-time or near-real-time queues, hourly and nightly refresh pipelines, and role-specific dashboards that different functions rely on for time-sensitive work. Advisors need risk and registration signals refreshed hourly or nightly, registrar staff need real-time queue visibility, and faculty-side compliance examples show the broader expectation that academic workflows cannot tolerate vague or delayed operational data.

Before IT will support the project, they need documented commitments in writing: dashboard load time < 2 seconds, event-driven or real-time refresh for operational bottlenecks < 30 seconds, alert delivery < 5 minutes, uptime SLA 99.9%, a named data owner for every major refresh pipeline, and a defined incident escalation process for launch week. They also need a clear boundary between AI recommendation layers and the official system-of-record transaction layers so that Albert can assist with decisions without silently mutating student records outside governed workflows. In change-management terms, IT will support the project only if the team proves the system is operable, observable, and governable.
5. NYU SENIOR LEADERSHIP — What metrics do they care about? What risks concern them? What must your change plan demonstrate to maintain their sponsorship?
Senior leadership will evaluate Albert’s redesign primarily through institutional outcomes, operational risk, and visible adoption. They will care about whether the new system reduces delayed graduation risk, improves registration flow, lowers waitlist pressure, reduces unresolved holds, and increases staff ability to act earlier on student risk. Department Chair and Registrar materials show that leadership-level concerns naturally center on required-course coverage, waitlist pressure, graduation-critical demand, and bottlenecks that threaten on-time completion.

The risks leadership will care about most are straightforward: low staff adoption, quiet return to manual workarounds, student distrust during registration, and a launch that increases noise without reducing friction. To maintain sponsorship, the change plan must show disciplined sequencing, role-specific training, measurable adoption targets, and explicit rollout gates. Leadership sponsorship should continue only if the project can show by month 3 that advisor weekly usage is >85%, registrar usage is >90%, unresolved holds are declining, queue processing is not worsening, and performance SLAs are being met. Leadership does not need promises that Albert is innovative; they need proof that the redesign improves institutional reliability without creating new operational fragility.

PART 3 · 30 min
Design Role-Specific Training Programs
1. ADVISOR TRAINING — Format, duration, specific skills covered, one hands-on practice scenario, and the definition of competency at training completion:
Advisor training will be delivered in three parts: a 90-minute instructor-led workshop, a 45-minute sandbox lab, and a one-page quick-reference guide. The training will cover: identifying high-risk advisees, interpreting registration readiness and hold ownership, using guided course planning, recognizing when degree audit anomalies require escalation, documenting overrides and follow-up consistently, and explaining AI-supported guidance without overstating certainty. These topics map directly to the advisor’s current workflow of intake, hold scan, degree audit review, course feasibility checking, exception handling, and final documentation.

The hands-on scenario will use a realistic case: a student with one active hold, one prerequisite problem, one likely degree audit discrepancy, and a tight graduation timeline. The advisor must identify the actual blocker order, create a primary and backup schedule, route the right escalation, and document the case correctly in Albert. Competency at completion means the advisor can finish this end-to-end case without relying on personal spreadsheets for the core workflow, can correctly distinguish advisory guidance from staff-controlled decisions, and can produce a clear student-facing explanation of next steps. Advisors who cannot complete the scenario accurately will receive follow-up coaching before go-live.
2. REGISTRAR / BURSAR STAFF TRAINING — Format, duration, workflow-specific exercises, and how you address the transition period concern. What support is available in the first 30 days post-launch?
Registrar and bursar-adjacent operational staff will receive a 2-hour live workflow session, a queue simulation lab, and launch-week office hours. Training content will focus on the exact operational decisions they make now: identifying the real blocker, determining whether capacity is the issue, deciding between raising a cap versus managing exceptions, prioritizing graduation-critical cases, applying the correct override path, and communicating next steps clearly. These exercises reflect the documented registrar workflow, where staff must review real blockers, assess room and faculty constraints, and process graduation-critical exceptions under time pressure.

Transition anxiety will be addressed directly. Staff will be told before training that the first 30 days include a protected support period: twice-daily issue huddles during peak registration, named escalation contacts, same-day triage of blocking defects, and permission to revert to approved backup procedures if a launch issue threatens queue throughput. Support available in the first 30 days includes daily office hours, a live issue log, rapid-reference job aids, and a formal stabilization review at day 7, day 14, and day 30.
3. STUDENT ONBOARDING — Design the in-app onboarding experience: what it teaches, how long it takes, and how you make it feel helpful rather than an obstacle:
Student onboarding will be built as a 2–3 minute in-app guided experience shown at first login, with a skip option after first completion. It will teach only the highest-value tasks: where to see holds, how to interpret blocker explanations, what counts as “your action needed,” what counts as “staff review pending,” and where to find help. The onboarding should feel like a practical guide, not a marketing overlay.

To keep it helpful rather than annoying, the guide will be task-based and short. Each step should answer a real student question: “Why can’t I register?”, “Who owns this issue?”, “What should I do next?”, and “How do I contact support?” The onboarding will be judged successful if a first-time user can identify their blocker and next step without opening a separate FAQ.
4. TRAIN THE TRAINER — How do you prepare department leads and advisors who will support their colleagues? Design a train-the-trainer process in detail:
The train-the-trainer model will prepare one advising lead and one operations lead per participating unit. Training happens in four steps. First, trainers attend a deep-dive functional briefing on the redesigned Albert workflows, decision boundaries, escalation routes, and known risks. Second, they receive sandbox access and complete a required set of edge-case scenarios. Third, they practice teaching the workflow to peers using a standard deck, FAQ sheet, and issue-escalation guide. Fourth, during launch they serve in rotating support coverage and submit daily feedback on recurring problems, training gaps, and workarounds being observed.

A trainer is certified only when they can do two things: complete a realistic case correctly themselves and coach another staff member through that same case without escalating unnecessarily. This makes the trainer cohort a real operational support layer rather than just early recipients of information.

4. Write your Change Management Executive Summary — a 3-paragraph statement for the NYU Provost explaining your implementation approach, why it will succeed, and how you will know it is working:
Albert’s redesign will succeed only if NYU treats it as a workflow change before treating it as a technology launch. Our implementation approach is phased, role-specific, and measurable. Leadership is informed first, frontline staff are trained before students see the system, and the first pilot focuses on the workflows where Albert’s weaknesses are most visible today: advising, registration blockers, waitlists, holds, and overrides. This sequencing reduces surprise, protects high-stakes operations, and ensures that the people most affected by the redesign help shape it before full rollout.

This approach will succeed because it is built around the actual work of each stakeholder group. Advisors need faster visibility into risk, registration readiness, and hold ownership. Registrar staff need a system that helps them identify the real blocker, manage queue pressure, and preserve fairness under time pressure. Students need plain-language explanations, clear next steps, and visible human review when cases are sensitive or ambiguous. IT needs performance SLAs, operational monitoring, and governed boundaries between AI guidance and official transactions. Instead of asking each group to simply “embrace change,” this plan ties the redesign to concrete workflow gains and protected transition support.

We will know the implementation is working if adoption and operational quality improve at the same time. That means advisor weekly usage above 85%, registrar usage above 90%, reduced unresolved holds, faster override processing, fewer student confusion tickets, and stable technical performance under load. It also means fewer quiet workarounds: less spreadsheet dependence, less email-based blocker diagnosis, and more confidence that Albert is the normal place where the work gets done. If those signals do not improve, rollout will pause and be corrected before expanding. In this plan, success is not defined by go-live alone. Success means Albert is trusted enough by staff and students to be used consistently in the moments that matter most.
