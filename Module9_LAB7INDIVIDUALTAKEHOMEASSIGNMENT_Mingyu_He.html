Specify Every AI Safety Guardrail
1. List every scope guardrail in your chatbot design. For each: topics blocked · enforcement mechanism · what the bot says when it hits the block:
Albert’s chatbot is intentionally designed as a read-and-assist system, not a decision-making or record-changing authority. First, the bot is blocked from directly changing any official student record, including enrollment, grades, holds, overrides, degree progress, billing, or schedule publication. This is enforced through read-only API permissions for the AI service account and a hard separation between AI-facing endpoints and official write actions. When the bot hits this boundary, it says, "I can explain the issue and prepare the next step, but I cannot change Albert's records directly. A human approver must complete that action.”

A second guardrail blocks the bot from making final academic or administrative decisions. The bot may summarize evidence about probation risk, graduation delays, prerequisite issues, or registration blockers, but it cannot decide probation, dismissal, graduation clearance, overload approval, or degree exceptions. This is enforced through workflow routing rules and policy controls that require authorized staff review before any outcome affects the student’s official record. When this block is triggered, the bot says, "I can summarize the evidence, but I cannot make a final academic decision. This case has been routed for human review.”

A third guardrail blocks the bot from handling restricted advice areas such as legal advice, immigration advice, medical advice, disability eligibility determinations, and official financial adjudication. The bot may identify that a related issue exists and direct the user to the correct office, but it cannot interpret those matters as an authority. This is enforced through intent classification, topic blocking, and approved routing responses. When triggered, the bot says, "This question requires an official university office or licensed professional. I can help direct you to the right office, but I cannot provide a binding answer here.”

A fourth guardrail prevents the bot from answering when evidence is weak, conflicting, outdated, or outside the user’s allowed scope. The system checks retrieval coverage, confidence score, and permission scope before producing an answer. If those checks fail, the bot is required to stop and escalate rather than guess. In that situation, it says, "I'm not confident enough to answer from the available records." I’m escalating this for human review.”

A fifth guardrail prevents the bot from seeing or using information beyond what the authenticated user is allowed to access. For example, an advisor can only see assigned advisees, a professor can only see students in their own sections, and a student can only see their own records. This is enforced through RBAC, row-level security, field-level masking, and permission-filtered retrieval in the RAG layer. When access is blocked, the bot says, "I can only access records you are permitted to see.”

A sixth guardrail prevents the bot from using protected or inappropriate personal attributes in recommendations or risk scoring. It does not use race, ethnicity, disability status, nationality proxies, or unrelated sensitive attributes to produce recommendations. This is enforced through a feature allowlist, column-level masking, bias review, and fairness testing. When relevant, the bot says: “I only use the information necessary for this workflow and do not use sensitive personal traits to make recommendations.”
2. What is your confidence threshold? State the exact percentage, what triggers escalation below it, and your rationale for that specific number:
Our confidence threshold is set at 85%. If the AI’s confidence falls below 85%, the system will not provide a definitive answer and will automatically escalate the case to a human reviewer. Escalation is also triggered when fewer than two authoritative retrieved sources support the answer, when the retrieved sources conflict with one another, when the question requires policy interpretation rather than factual retrieval, or when the answer could affect a student’s official record.

The rationale for setting the threshold at 85% is that Albert operates in a high-stakes academic environment where an incorrect answer can affect registration, grading, progression, or student trust. A lower threshold would increase the chance that the system sounds confident while being wrong. By setting the bar at 85%, the design favors safety, traceability, and escalation over speed. This aligns with the overall project principle that Albert AI should support human judgment, not replace it.
3. How does your RAG system prevent hallucination? Describe the specific technical mechanism — not just 'it uses RAG' but how answers are constrained to retrieved content only:
Albert’s RAG system prevents hallucination by restricting the model to permission-filtered, retrieved evidence rather than allowing it to answer freely from general model knowledge. Every request begins with user authentication, and the session token carries role and scope information such as user role, assigned students, taught sections, school, and term. The retrieval engine then searches only within sources that the current user is authorized to access.

The retrieved content is also constrained by metadata such as student ID, section ID, role scope, module, and policy version. This means the model only receives specific record excerpts, approved policy text, and structured workflow data that match the user’s access rights. It does not receive unrestricted database access and cannot browse across records outside the approved scope.

After retrieval, the system checks whether the answer is sufficiently supported by the returned evidence. It validates retrieval score, source consistency, document freshness, and whether the requested answer would imply a record-changing action. If support is incomplete, contradictory, or too weak, the model is not allowed to improvise. Instead, it returns an escalation response. In high-risk workflows, the system uses template-bounded generation, meaning the answer is built from structured rule outputs first and then verbalized in plain language. This design ensures that the bot is constrained to retrieved content only, rather than generating unsupported conclusions.
4. For which AI-influenced decisions must a human review before it affects a student's record? List every human-in-the-loop requirement in your design:
Human review is required for every AI-influenced action that could materially affect a student’s official academic or administrative record. This includes clearing any hold that is not explicitly advisor-clearable, approving enrollment overrides, granting prerequisite exceptions, approving overloads, changing degree requirements or substitutions, updating transfer credit decisions, or changing milestone status in a degree audit.

Human review is also mandatory for any academic standing action influenced by AI, such as probation review, graduation clearance, dismissal-related recommendations, or other progression decisions. In the teaching workflow, AI may flag grading issues, academic risk, hidden grades, or gradebook inconsistencies, but a professor must review and approve any final grade submission, grade correction, incomplete, regrade, or academic integrity outcome before it becomes official.

In registrar and planning workflows, AI may identify capacity bottlenecks, waitlist pressure, or graduation-critical course demand, but a human must review any cap increase, override execution, section addition, schedule publication, or faculty-load decision. In financial workflows, AI may identify that a bursar-related hold exists, but only authorized bursar staff can adjudicate or release it. In short, the AI can assist with diagnosis, prioritization, and drafts, but any action that changes the system of record must go through an authorized human reviewer.

Build Your FERPA Compliance Matrix
1. FERPA Compliance Matrix — For EACH AI data flow: Feature/Data Flow · FERPA provision governing it · How your design ensures compliance · Any vendor agreements required:
For advisor intake triage, where the AI accesses student identity, enrollment status, holds, and degree progress, the governing FERPA principle is the school official exception with legitimate educational interest. Compliance is ensured by limiting access to authenticated advisors working only with assigned students, logging access, and restricting the AI to read-only support. The required vendor agreement is a FERPA addendum and institutional data processing agreement.

For degree-audit support and academic risk summaries, the same FERPA principle applies because the AI is helping authorized staff perform institutional duties. Compliance is ensured through role-based access, permission-filtered retrieval, evidence-based outputs, and human review before any record-changing action. The vendor must operate under NYU’s direct control through a FERPA addendum and DPA.

For registrar blocker diagnosis and override triage, FERPA applies because the AI is using education records such as enrollment attempts, holds, prerequisites, and degree progress. Compliance is ensured by limiting this function to registrar-authorized staff, logging every access, and requiring a human registrar to approve any override or exception. This also requires a FERPA addendum and DPA.

For professor-facing grading-risk alerts and gradebook audits, FERPA applies because grades, submissions, and course participation are education records. Compliance is ensured by restricting access to instructors and approved instructional staff for their own sections only and by requiring the professor to validate any grade-related action before anything is posted. This data flow also requires a FERPA addendum and DPA.

For department chair forecasting and planning tools, FERPA applies when individual student education records are used, but the system should default to aggregate or de-identified outputs whenever named student detail is not necessary. Compliance is ensured by showing identifiable student data only to authorized users with a legitimate educational interest and keeping aggregated planning views de-identified whenever possible. This also requires the vendor to operate under NYU’s contractual FERPA controls.
2. Name your third-party AI API. What data agreement is required (BAA/FERPA addendum/DPA)? Does the standard tier include it? If not, what is your solution?
The third-party AI API in our design is Azure OpenAI Service deployed inside an NYU-controlled enterprise Azure environment. Because the system touches student education records, the required agreement is not just a generic cloud contract. NYU would need a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) together with a FERPA addendum or school-official agreement that places the vendor under NYU’s direct control and limits use of the data strictly to authorized institutional purposes.

The standard tier alone is not sufficient for this kind of university use case if it does not explicitly include FERPA-specific contractual protections and governance language. Our solution is to use only the enterprise procurement path, not a consumer or public AI tier, and require NYU legal, privacy, and procurement teams to finalize institution-level FERPA terms before production deployment. This ensures that the AI vendor is contractually bound to confidentiality, use limitation, deletion/return obligations, incident notification, and audit cooperation.

4. How does a student exercise their right to review and contest an AI-influenced decision about their academic record? Describe the complete process step by step:
If a student believes an AI-influenced outcome is incorrect or unfair, the student can select a “Request Human Review” option directly from the Albert interface. This opens a review request that shows the decision summary, the office responsible for the workflow, and the type of data that was used. The case is then routed to the appropriate human office, such as advising, registrar, bursar, or instructor review.

Next, an authorized NYU staff member re-examines the student’s actual education record, the applicable policy, the AI-generated output, and any supporting or missing documentation. If the issue is simply an operational error, the office corrects the outcome and logs the correction. If the student believes the underlying record itself is inaccurate, misleading, or incomplete, the student can formally request amendment of the education record through NYU’s FERPA process.

If NYU decides not to amend the record, the student must be informed of the right to a formal hearing. At that point, the student can present evidence, and the university must issue a written decision. This process ensures that the student has a clear path to both human review and formal contest rights whenever an AI-influenced workflow touches the academic record.

Complete RBAC Design & Security Architecture
1. COMPLETE RBAC MATRIX — For all 6 roles (Student, Advisor, Registrar, Bursar, IT Admin, AI System): what data they can read · what they can write · what is always off-limits. Name specific Albert modules:
The student role may read only their own modules, including their student center, schedule, degree audit, holds summary, billing summary, and AI-generated explanations related to their own record. A student may write only to self-service areas such as course cart selections, form submissions, preferences, and contact updates. A student is always blocked from other students’ data, faculty notes, override approvals, cap changes, and administrative logs.

The Advisor role may read assigned advisees’ records, including enrollment status, degree audit, holds, registration information, advising notes, and advisor-facing dashboards. Advisors may write advising notes, outreach actions, draft case records, and advisor-clearable hold actions where policy permits. Advisors are always blocked from full bursar decision-making, registrar-only override execution, final grade posting, and university-wide administrative security controls.

The registrar role may read student registration data, advisement reports, override queues, section capacity information, waitlists, and enrollment control modules. Registrars may write approved override actions, permission entries, cap changes, enrollment corrections, and section-level enrollment controls. They are always blocked from private instructional grading notes, full bursar financial adjudication, and IAM/security administration.

The bursar role may read billing ledgers, payment plans, refund status, student identity basics, and financial hold information. Bursar staff may write financial hold actions, payment status updates, and billing comments. They are always blocked from grades, advising notes, academic standing tools, and registrar override functions.

The IT Admin role may read identity and access management settings, API health, audit logs, integration status, and system configuration metadata. IT administrators may write role mappings, authentication settings, service-account controls, secrets management, network controls, and logging configuration. They are always blocked from routine browsing of student education-record content unless there is a formally approved break-glass security incident process.

The AI system role is read-only and limited to permission-filtered retrieval from approved Albert modules such as degree audit, registration, holds, advising, course, and Brightspace-linked records only when the authenticated user is entitled to see them. The AI system cannot write directly to authoritative Albert records. It may only produce non-authoritative drafts or summaries in controlled workflow spaces. It is always blocked from unrestricted databases, private keys, admin secrets, and direct record changes.
2. SECURITY ARCHITECTURE — All 5 layers with specific technical commitments: (1) Authentication & Identity, (2) Encryption at rest/in transit, (3) API access controls, (4) Audit logging retention, (5) Incident response playbook:
The first security layer is authentication and identity. Albert uses NYU single sign-on with multi-factor authentication, short-lived session tokens, and separate service identities for staff, integrations, and the AI system. High-risk administrator access uses stronger controls and enhanced logging.

The second layer is encryption at rest and in transit. All traffic between users, Albert services, and integrated systems is encrypted in transit using strong TLS. Data stored in databases, indexes, backups, and log archives is encrypted at rest using enterprise encryption standards. Encryption keys are managed centrally, rotated regularly, and protected through secure key management practices.

The third layer is API Access Control. Every request to Albert services passes through an API gateway that enforces role-based access, scope checks, row-level security, field masking, rate limiting, and private network rules. The AI service account is restricted to read-only scopes and cannot call direct write endpoints.

The fourth layer is audit logging and retention. Albert records authentication events, retrieval requests, prompts, retrieved sources, generated responses, approvals, record changes, role changes, and break-glass access. Logs are retained long enough to support compliance, appeals, investigations, and post-incident analysis.

The fifth layer is the Incident Response Playbook. If a security event occurs, the system follows a defined process: detect the incident, contain the issue, revoke tokens or disable affected AI paths, preserve logs, determine the scope of data exposure, notify appropriate NYU security and privacy leaders, and complete remediation and post-incident review. This creates an end-to-end security model that protects both the technical system and the student record lifecycle.
3. How do you technically enforce that the AI system can never access data beyond what the authenticated user is permitted to see? Describe the enforcement mechanism:
This is enforced through a layered permission model. After a user signs in through NYU SSO, the session token contains role and scope claims such as whether the user is a student, advisor, registrar, professor, or bursar, and what specific students, sections, or academic units they are allowed to access. The AI never decides this scope for itself. It inherits the scope from Albert’s access-control system.

Before retrieval happens, a policy engine checks the request and resolves exactly what the user is allowed to see. The retrieval system then applies mandatory filters using ACL metadata such as student ID, section ID, role scope, and module scope. As a result, an advisor only retrieves assigned advisees, a professor only retrieves their own course rosters, and a student only retrieves their own record. If the user does not have permission, retrieval returns nothing.

Finally, the AI runtime itself is read-only and cannot bypass Albert’s API gateway. It does not query the raw database directly. Instead, it receives only the filtered evidence package that the gateway and policy layer have already approved. This technically guarantees that the AI can never access data beyond the authenticated user’s permitted scope.

Bias Mitigation & Regulatory Compliance
1. BIAS MITIGATION PLAN — For at least 3 specific AI features: (1) Feature name · (2) Specific bias risk · (3) Pre-deployment testing · (4) Ongoing monitoring · (5) Remediation if bias is detected:
For the advisor probation and persistence risk model, the specific bias risk is that historically disadvantaged groups, transfer students, part-time students, or international students could be over-flagged because the model learns old patterns of institutional inequality rather than true student need. Before deployment, the model should be tested for subgroup calibration, false-positive rates, and proxy-variable risk. Ongoing monitoring should compare model performance across student groups each term. If bias is detected, the model should be paused, proxy features removed, thresholds recalibrated, and the workflow temporarily reverted to rule-based alerts until fairness improves.

For the registrar override prioritization feature, the specific bias risk is that students from better-supported programs or students who are more assertive in communication may appear more urgent and receive more approvals. Before deployment, override decisions should be audited across matched student profiles to test consistency and remove nonessential free-text influence. Ongoing monitoring should review approval-rate variance by program, cohort, and advisor. If bias is found, the process should require standardized evidence fields, second-level review for edge cases, and re-auditing of past outcomes.

For the professor course-risk feature, the bias risk is that engagement-based variables may unfairly penalize students with accommodations, multilingual learners, or students facing temporary technical barriers. Before deployment, feature sets should be reviewed to separate true academic performance from noisy behavioral proxies, and false positives should be tested across sections and student subgroups. Ongoing monitoring should compare flags with actual student outcomes and instructor confirmations. If bias appears, problematic features should be reduced or removed, thresholds recalibrated, and professor review made mandatory before any outreach escalation.

For the grading consistency monitor, the bias risk is that small-sample variations or context differences across sections could cause the system to over-flag certain graders unfairly. Before deployment, thresholds should be validated against historical data and human-reviewed calibration samples. Ongoing monitoring should track sustained drift rather than isolated outliers. If the tool is over-flagging, thresholds should be adjusted, small-sample cases excluded, and no corrective action should occur without faculty review.
2. REGULATORY COMPLIANCE SUMMARY — How your design addresses: FERPA · NY SHIELD Act · WCAG 2.1 Accessibility · Third-party vendor certifications (SOC 2, etc.):
The design addresses FERPA by limiting AI access to authenticated users with a legitimate educational interest, keeping the vendor under institutional control through contract, logging access to education records, and ensuring that students retain the right to inspect, review, and contest records that influence university decisions. The AI does not act as an independent decision-maker and does not directly alter official student records.

The design addresses the NY SHIELD Act by implementing reasonable administrative, technical, and physical safeguards. These include named security ownership, access controls, encryption, monitoring, incident response procedures, vendor oversight, and ongoing risk assessment. These controls are designed to reduce unauthorized access, misuse, and disclosure of student-related data.

The design addresses WCAG 2.1 Accessibility by requiring that Albert AI interfaces meet accessibility standards such as keyboard navigation, screen-reader compatibility, sufficient contrast, understandable error states, and non-color-only alerts. This ensures that the AI-supported workflows are usable by the full NYU community, including users with disabilities.

The design also requires that third-party vendors provide appropriate security and assurance documentation, such as SOC 2 or equivalent audit evidence, along with university-approved privacy and security terms. In practice, the AI vendor must meet NYU’s enterprise procurement, privacy, and compliance requirements before any production use involving education records.
3. Write your Security & Privacy Executive Summary — a 3-paragraph statement for non-technical NYU senior leadership explaining how your design protects students in plain language:
Albert AI is designed to make university work faster and clearer without taking decision authority away from NYU staff. The system helps users interpret records, identify blockers, prioritize cases, and prepare drafts, but it does not independently change official student records. Any action that affects registration, grades, holds, degree progress, billing, or schedule publication still requires authorized human review and approval.

From a privacy perspective, the system is built around the principle of minimum necessary access. The AI only sees the records that the current authenticated user is already allowed to see, and only for legitimate educational purposes tied to that workflow. Access is logged, the user is informed when education-record data is being used, and students retain the right to request human review and contest inaccurate record-related outcomes through established university procedures.

From a security perspective, Albert AI uses strong identity controls, encryption, permission-checked APIs, audit logging, role-based access control, and incident response procedures to protect student data. The overall design also supports compliance with FERPA, the NY SHIELD Act, accessibility standards, and enterprise vendor security requirements. In plain language, the goal is simple: Albert AI can help NYU work smarter, but it cannot act beyond the rules, beyond the user’s permissions, or beyond human oversight.
